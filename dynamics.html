What is Dynamics?

Dynamics is the branch of mechanics that studies how forces cause objects to move and accelerate. Mechanical engineers use dynamics to analyze the motion of machines, vehicles, robots, and other mechanical systems. By understanding the relationship between forces and motion, engineers can design systems that operate safely, efficiently, and predictably.

Forces and Motion Combined

Dynamics is the branch of mechanics that studies how forces affect motion. Unlike statics, where forces are balanced and acceleration is zero, dynamics focuses on situations where forces are unbalanced and cause objects to accelerate.

Dynamics combines ideas from both statics and kinematics. Statics teaches us about forces. Kinematics teaches us about motion. Dynamics explains how forces create motion.

1. Newton's First Law

Newton's First Law states that an object will remain at rest or continue moving at a constant velocity unless acted upon by an unbalanced force.

This concept is called inertia.

Examples:

  • A book remains on a table until pushed.
  • A hockey puck slides until friction slows it down.
  • A car continues moving unless braking forces act on it.

2. Inertia

Inertia is an object's tendency to resist changes in motion. The greater the mass of an object, the greater its inertia.

This means heavier objects require more force to start moving, stop moving, or change direction.

Examples:

  • A loaded truck is harder to push than an empty cart.
  • A large machine takes longer to stop after being turned off.
  • A passenger lurches forward when a car suddenly brakes.

Understanding inertia helps engineers design safer vehicles, machines, and transportation systems.

3. Newton's Second Law

Newton's Second Law explains how forces create acceleration.

The equation is:

F = ma

  • F = Net Force (N)
  • m = Mass (kg)
  • a = Acceleration (m/s²)

This means acceleration depends on both force and mass.

  • More force produces more acceleration.
  • More mass produces less acceleration.

For example, a shopping cart accelerates more easily than a fully loaded truck because the truck has much greater mass.

4. Newton's Third Law

Newton's Third Law states:

For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction.

Whenever one object exerts a force on another object, the second object exerts an equal force back.

Examples:

  • Walking forward by pushing backward on the ground.
  • Rocket engines pushing exhaust downward while the rocket moves upward.
  • A swimmer pushing water backward to move forward.

5. Net Force

The net force is the sum of all forces acting on an object.

If the net force equals zero:

  • No acceleration occurs.
  • The object remains in equilibrium.

If the net force does not equal zero:

  • The object accelerates.

Examples:

  • 50 N right and 50 N left = 0 N net force.
  • 80 N right and 20 N left = 60 N net force right.

6. Forces as Vectors

Forces are vector quantities, meaning they have both magnitude and direction.

Because direction matters, engineers often break forces into horizontal and vertical components.

For example, a force acting at an angle may have:

  • A horizontal component (Fx)
  • A vertical component (Fy)

Analyzing components allows engineers to determine how each part of a force contributes to motion.

This process is used constantly in mechanical engineering, civil engineering, and aerospace engineering.

7. Free Body Diagrams

Engineers often use Free Body Diagrams (FBDs) to visualize forces.

A Free Body Diagram shows all forces acting on an object.

Common forces include:

  • Weight (W)
  • Normal Force (N)
  • Tension (T)
  • Friction (F)
  • Applied Force

Free Body Diagrams are one of the most important tools in engineering and physics.

8. Why Free Body Diagrams Matter

Free Body Diagrams are one of the most powerful tools used by engineers and physicists.

Before solving almost any force problem, engineers first create a Free Body Diagram.

The diagram helps identify:

  • All forces acting on the object
  • Force directions
  • Missing forces
  • Possible acceleration directions

Without Free Body Diagrams, complex engineering problems become much more difficult to analyze correctly.

9. Friction

Friction opposes motion between surfaces.

The friction equation is:

F = μN

  • F = Friction Force
  • μ = Coefficient of Friction
  • N = Normal Force

Friction can:

  • Slow objects down.
  • Prevent slipping.
  • Create heat.

10. Static vs. Kinetic Friction

There are two main types of friction.

Static Friction acts when objects are not moving relative to one another.

Static friction prevents motion from starting.

Kinetic Friction acts when surfaces are already sliding against one another.

Kinetic friction usually has a smaller magnitude than static friction.

Examples:

  • Pushing a heavy box requires extra force to start moving it.
  • Once the box begins sliding, less force may be needed to keep it moving.

11. Types of Acceleration

Acceleration can occur in several ways.

  • Speeding up
  • Slowing down
  • Changing direction

Many students think acceleration only means speeding up, but any change in velocity creates acceleration.

A car moving around a curve at constant speed is still accelerating because its direction changes continuously.

12. Circular Motion (Intro)

Objects moving in circles constantly change direction.

Even if speed stays constant, changing direction means acceleration exists.

This acceleration is called centripetal acceleration.

Examples:

  • Cars driving around curves.
  • Roller coasters.
  • Satellites orbiting Earth.

13. Momentum

Momentum describes the quantity of motion possessed by an object.

The equation for momentum is:

p = mv

  • p = Momentum
  • m = Mass
  • v = Velocity

Momentum increases when either mass or velocity increases.

A fast-moving truck has much more momentum than a bicycle because its mass is significantly greater.

Momentum is important because changing momentum requires force.

Engineers study momentum when designing:

  • Vehicle safety systems
  • Airbags
  • Seatbelts
  • Crash structures
  • Protective equipment

Understanding momentum helps engineers reduce injuries and improve safety.

14. Work and Energy (Introduction)

Dynamics often connects to energy concepts.

Work occurs whenever a force causes displacement.

The basic equation for work is:

Work = Force × Distance

Energy is the ability to do work.

Machines constantly convert energy into motion.

Examples include:

  • Electric motors turning shafts
  • Cars converting fuel into motion
  • Roller coasters converting potential energy into kinetic energy

Later engineering courses study these relationships in much greater detail.

15. Why Dynamics Matters

Dynamics is one of the most important subjects in mechanical engineering because machines rarely remain motionless.

Engineers use dynamics when designing:

  • Cars
  • Aircraft
  • Robots
  • Industrial Machines
  • Roller Coasters
  • Manufacturing Systems

Understanding dynamics allows engineers to predict how systems move, accelerate, stop, and respond to forces.

Example Problems

Problem 1: Finding Net Force

A box is pushed with 80 N to the right while friction applies 20 N to the left. Find the net force.

Step 1: Identify the force directions

Right = positive, left = negative

Step 2: Add the forces

Fnet = 80 - 20

Step 3: Calculate

Fnet = 60 N

Answer: The net force is 60 N to the right.

Problem 2: Using Newton's Second Law

A 10 kg object experiences a net force of 50 N. Find the acceleration of the object.

Step 1: Use Newton's Second Law

F = ma

Step 2: Rearrange to solve for acceleration

a = F / m

Step 3: Substitute values

a = 50 / 10

Step 4: Calculate

a = 5 m/s²

Answer: The object accelerates at 5 m/s².

Problem 3: Calculating Friction Force

A box has a normal force of 300 N. The coefficient of friction is 0.25. Find the friction force.

Step 1: Use the friction formula

Ff = μN

Step 2: Substitute values

Ff = 0.25 × 300

Step 3: Calculate

Ff = 75 N

Answer: The friction force is 75 N.

Problem 4: Calculating Momentum

A 1,200 kg car is moving at 20 m/s. Find the momentum of the car.

Step 1: Use the momentum formula

p = mv

Step 2: Substitute values

p = 1200 × 20

Step 3: Calculate

p = 24,000 kg·m/s

Answer: The car's momentum is 24,000 kg·m/s.

Real World Applications of Dynamics

Dynamics is used whenever engineers study objects that are moving or changing motion. It helps explain how forces, mass, acceleration, velocity, momentum, and energy affect machines, vehicles, structures, and everyday mechanical systems.

1. Cars Accelerating and Braking

Cars use dynamics when they speed up, slow down, or turn. Engineers study the forces between the tires and road to design safer braking systems and better handling.

Concepts used:

  • Newton's Second Law: force causes acceleration.
  • Friction: tires need grip to speed up and stop.
  • Acceleration: change in velocity over time.

2. Roller Coasters

Roller coasters use dynamics as the cart speeds up, slows down, climbs hills, drops, and turns. Engineers design the track so passengers experience motion safely.

Concepts used:

  • Kinetic energy: energy from motion.
  • Potential energy: energy from height.
  • Centripetal force: force needed for curved motion.

3. Elevators

Elevators use dynamics when they start moving, stop, and carry passengers. The cable tension changes depending on whether the elevator is accelerating upward, accelerating downward, or moving at constant speed.

Concepts used:

  • Free-body diagrams: forces on the elevator car.
  • Tension: pulling force in the cable.
  • Acceleration: affects the apparent weight of passengers.

4. Robotic Arms

Robotic arms use dynamics when motors move links, joints, and tools. Engineers must account for mass, acceleration, torque, and changing loads.

Concepts used:

  • Torque: rotational force at joints.
  • Moment of inertia: resistance to rotational acceleration.
  • Angular acceleration: change in rotational speed.

5. Sports Equipment

Dynamics appears in sports when balls are thrown, bats swing, bicycles move, or athletes jump. Engineers use these ideas to improve equipment performance.

Concepts used:

  • Projectile motion: motion through the air.
  • Impulse: force applied over time.
  • Momentum: mass times velocity.

6. Cranes and Moving Loads

Cranes do not only lift static loads. When a load starts, stops, or swings, dynamics becomes important because motion can increase forces in the system.

Concepts used:

  • Tension: force in the cable.
  • Pendulum motion: swinging loads.
  • Acceleration: changes the forces during lifting.

7. Engines and Rotating Parts

Engines, gears, shafts, fans, and wheels all involve rotating motion. Engineers use dynamics to reduce vibration and make rotating parts safer.

Concepts used:

  • Angular velocity: rotational speed.
  • Torque: causes rotational motion.
  • Rotational inertia: resistance to changes in rotation.

8. Vehicle Suspension Systems

Suspension systems help vehicles handle bumps, turns, and uneven roads. Springs and dampers control motion so the vehicle stays stable and comfortable.

Concepts used:

  • Oscillation: repeated up-and-down motion.
  • Damping: reducing vibration over time.
  • Spring force: restoring force from suspension springs.

9. Aircraft and Rockets

Aircraft and rockets rely heavily on dynamics because their motion changes due to thrust, gravity, drag, lift, and changing mass.

Concepts used:

  • Thrust: force that pushes the vehicle forward.
  • Drag: air resistance opposing motion.
  • Newton's laws: explain how forces change motion.

10. Future Experiment Demonstrations

This section can later include your own demonstrations, such as rolling a toy car down a ramp, swinging a pendulum, measuring stopping distance, or comparing how mass affects acceleration.

Concepts used:

  • Force and acceleration: how motion changes.
  • Energy transfer: potential energy changing into kinetic energy.
  • Momentum: motion depending on mass and velocity.

  • Increasing the ramp height increases the object's speed.
  • Potential energy changes into kinetic energy.
  • Friction and air resistance slow the object down.
  • Mass, force, acceleration, velocity, and energy are all connected in dynamics.
